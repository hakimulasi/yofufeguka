    FAQs About Nelly Akopian-Tamarina

    Who is Nelly Akopian-Tamarina?

    Nelly Akopian-Tamarina is a Russian pianist of Armenian origin, known for her profound interpretations of classical piano works, particularly those of Brahms, Schumann, and Chopin.

    What are some significant achievements in her career?

    She won the Gold Medal at the 1963 Robert Schumann International Competition and received the Robert Schumann Prize in 1974. Her career includes performing at major venues like Wigmore Hall and the Moscow Conservatory.

    When did she make her London debut?

    She made her London debut in 1983 at the Queen Elizabeth Hall, where she performed works by Schumann and Chopin.

    What are some of her most notable recordings?

    Her discography includes critically acclaimed albums such as Slavonic Reflections and a Brahms collection featuring Variations and Fugue on a theme by Handel and Ballades, Op. 10.

    What is her educational background?

    She studied at the Moscow Central Music School under Anaida Sumbatyan and later at the Moscow Conservatory with Alexander Goldenweiser and Dmitri Bashkirov.

    Has she worked in educational roles?

    Yes, from 1989 to 2006, she held roles such as an artistic consultant at the Prague Conservatory and taught masterclasses at the Pálffy Palace. She also held appointments in London at the Royal Academy of Music and the Royal College of Music.

    What are some unique aspects of her performance style?

    Her performances are known for their personal and introspective qualities, often bringing out the deeper emotional content of the music, which has been described as deeply lyrical and spellbinding.

    What challenges has she faced when interpreting Brahms?

    She has described the challenge of entering Brahms’ musical world, noting the depth and reverence she feels towards his compositions, likening it to being in awe within a gothic cathedral.

    What are some of her favorite venues for performance?

    She prefers performing in older concert halls due to their acoustic properties and aesthetic character, with favorites including the Dvořák Hall of the Rudolfinum in Prague, the Moscow Conservatory, and London’s Wigmore Hall.
